# Project Overview

High employee attrition creates operational and financial challenges for organizations through recruitment costs, productivity loss, and workforce instability. This project explores the workforce attrition patterns using HR data to identify trends related to employee experience, demographics, overtime, tenure, and job satisfaction.

The dashboard was designed to give answers to the skateholder's business question such as, which employee groups experience higher attrition, the factors that may influence employee turnover, the workforce composition across departments and roles, and the relationships between employee satisfaction and retention. I created a three page interactive Power BI dashboard that gives answers those business question. 

# Tools Used

Power BI was used to create an interactive dashboard that is easy for my non technical skateholders to understand.

DAX was used for KPI calculations and custom measures.

SQL was used for data preparation, view creation, and data analysis.

Excel was used for data cleaning and auditing.

# Key Insights

The organization recorded 237 attrition cases from a workforce of 1,470 employees, resulting in an attrition rate of 16.12%. This indicates that employee turnover is a meaningful workforce issue rather than an isolated occurrence.

Employees working overtime accounted for 177 of the 237 attrition cases (74.52%). This suggests that workload pressure and extended working hours may be strongly contributing to employee turnover.

The Sales department recorded the highest attrition rate at 20.6%, with Human Resources following at 19.1%. Although Research & Development had a larger workforce population, its attrition rate remained lower at 13.8%, highlighting the importance of evaluating attrition rates instead of raw employee counts alone.

Employees under the age of 25 recorded the highest attrition rate at 39.2%, significantly higher than older workforce groups. This may suggest that younger employees are more likely to leave due to career mobility, role mismatch, or limited long term engagement.

Male employees accounted for 150 attrition cases (63.29%), while female employees accounted for 87 cases (36.71%). While this does not confirm causation, it highlights a workforce segment that may require deeper retention analysis.

Employees with job satisfaction scores of 1 and 2 showed the highest attrition concentration. This supports the relationship between employee dissatisfaction and increased turnover risk.

Attrition patterns were highest among employees with fewer years since their last promotion, then declined across later promotion gap groups. This suggests that promotion timing and early career progression may influence retention outcomes.

Work life balance analysis showed that employees without attrition consistently reported stronger balance levels compared to employees who left the company. This suggests that work life balance may play a role in employee retention stability.

Sales Executive and Research Scientist represented the largest workforce role groups, indicating that workforce planning and retention strategies may have the greatest organizational impact when focused on these roles.

Life Sciences and Medical education backgrounds represented the largest workforce segments, showing that the organization’s workforce is heavily concentrated in technical and health related disciplines.

# Recommendations

Conduct a workload and scheduling review for overtime heavy teams, particularly within Sales. Since 177 of the 237 attrition cases came from employees working overtime, management should identify teams consistently exceeding normal workload thresholds and evaluate staffing gaps, unrealistic targets, or uneven task allocation.

Introduce retention focused performance reviews for employees under 25 within their first 12-18 months. With this group recording the highest attrition rate at 39.2%, the organization should implement structured onboarding follow-ups, mentorship pairing, and career progression discussions early in the employee lifecycle.

Perform department level attrition audits in Sales and Human Resources, where attrition rates exceeded 19%. Instead of using company wide retention policies, HR teams should compare manager turnover patterns, workload distribution, promotion frequency, and employee feedback scores within these departments to identify localized retention issues.

Create an early warning retention framework using satisfaction indicators. Since employees with satisfaction scores of 1 and 2 showed the strongest attrition concentration, HR teams could flag low satisfaction employees for intervention through manager check ins, workload reviews, or internal mobility discussions before resignation risk increases.

Reassess promotion pacing and internal mobility opportunities for employees in early to mid tenure stages. Attrition patterns were highest among employees with fewer years since promotion, suggesting that employees may disengage when career progression expectations are not met quickly enough.

Expand flexible work and employee wellness initiatives in teams showing lower work life balance outcomes. Since employees with weaker balance indicators showed stronger attrition patterns, workload flexibility and recovery support may help stabilize retention.

Prioritize retention planning for Sales Executives and Research Scientists, as these roles represent some of the largest workforce groups. Small increases in turnover within these roles could create disproportionately larger operational and recruitment impacts compared to smaller workforce segments.

Align workforce development programs with the organization’s dominant educational backgrounds, particularly Life Sciences and Medical disciplines. Since these groups make up a significant portion of the workforce, targeted technical development and specialization pathways may improve long term employee engagement and retention.
